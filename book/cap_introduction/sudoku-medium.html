1. Naked Pairs and Triplets

Look for two cells within the same row, column, or 3x3 box. If both of these cells have only the same two numbers as candidates, those two numbers are a "Naked Pair." This means those two numbers must go in those two cells, and you can remove them as candidates from all other cells in that row, column, or box.

The same idea works for Naked Triplets: three cells with only the same three candidates.

2. Box/Line Reduction (Pointing Pairs/Triples)

Focus on a single 3x3 box. Look for a candidate that appears only along one row or one column within that box. If you find such a candidate, you can eliminate it from the rest of that row or column outside the box.

Step 1: Finding Naked Pairs/Triplets (Using Pencil Marks)

First, fill in the pencil marks (candidates) for all empty cells. This is crucial for finding Naked Pairs/Triplets and for Box/Line Reduction. After you've filled them in, look carefully at the grid. It is important to imagine you are filling the candidates.

After carefully reviewing and considering the candidates, we can spot a Naked Pair. In the first box we can see in 2nd column that the only candidates are 1 and 3( what is imporant is that they are the same for both cells), so we can eliminate them as candidates for the rest of the box.

Step 2: Applying Box/Line Reduction (Using Pencil Marks)

Now we can search a candidate inside a box that appears only on one col or one row. In the same first box the number 3 appears only on column, this tell us that the number must go one of those two cells, letting us eliminate it as a candidate from the rest of the second column.

So we can eliminate the number 3 from the rest of the second column.

After finding all the Singles you can, look for Naked Pairs/Triplets and Box/Line Reduction. These techniques often help you find more Singles! Remember to fill candidates first.
